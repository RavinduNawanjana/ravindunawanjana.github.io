  <h3>The Role of Stock Exchanges in Promoting the Banking Sector in Adopting Green Finance: A Global Analysis with Insights from Emerging Markets</h3>

  <p>
    This study investigates how stock exchanges can promote green finance adoption among banks
    globally, combining a synthesis of 64 sources with primary survey data from financial
    practitioners across emerging markets including India, Malaysia, Ghana, and Sri Lanka. Four
    interdependent drivers of adoption are identified &mdash; market regulations, market
    mechanisms, awareness, and incentives. Notably, IMF/IOSCO data show that only 49% of
    regulators in low-income economies maintain supportive market regulations, compared with
    85% in high-income OECD nations. The findings indicate that stock exchanges must transition
    from voluntary guidance to binding frameworks mandating ESG disclosure and green bond
    standards for listed banks, supported by targeted tax incentives, formalised peer exchange
    partnerships, and standardised carbon market disclosure.
  </p>

  <h3>The Role of Multilateral Climate Funds in Shaping the Architecture of Climate Finance: An Analysis of Blended Finance and Institutional Collaboration among MDBs, Central Banks, and Private Capital across the G77 and China</h3>

  <p>
    This study examines the role of Multilateral Climate Funds (MCFs) in shaping climate
    finance architecture across the G77 and China, focusing on four determinants: institutional
    collaboration, blended finance deployment, policy and regulatory alignment, and
    transparency. I integrate quantitative data from 103 sustainable finance specialists with
    qualitative insights from eight semi-structured interviews with representatives of MDBs,
    central banks, and private capital institutions. Blended finance mechanisms emerge as the
    strongest predictor of climate finance architecture, acting as the primary catalyst for
    mobilising private capital &mdash; though their effectiveness is constrained by
    institutional fragmentation, bureaucratic silos between vertical funds and MDBs, and
    regulatory dissonance across financial systems. The paper introduces the
    <strong>Institutional Reinforcement Cycle</strong>, a recursive model theorising the
    interdependence of institutional, financial, and regulatory variables in determining
    climate finance effectiveness, and provides policy recommendations for evolving MCFs and
    MDBs beyond ODA and bilateral co-financing towards coordinated platforms that de-risk
    private investment at scale.
  </p>
